Original terminology invented by R U Narasimham to bring fresh perspectives to understanding English

🔀

Versonym

A multipurpose word — serves as more than one part of speech

There are approximately 1,70,000 words in the English dictionary. Many of them can function as different parts of speech depending on context. R U Narasimham coined the term "Versonym" for such multipurpose words.

Example: WATER

Noun People need drinking water.
Adjective Crane is a water bird.
Verb The gardeners water the plants regularly.

Since "water" can be used as a noun, adjective, or verb, it is a Versonym — a multipurpose word.

Exclunym

A single-purpose word — belongs to only one part of speech

The opposite of a Versonym. An Exclunym is a word that serves a single, exclusive purpose — it can only be used as one part of speech.

Examples

Noun only Javelin — a long spear thrown in competitive sport. Cannot be used as any other part of speech.
Adjective only Dignified — having a serious manner worthy of respect.
Adverb only Very — used for emphasis, always functions as an adverb.

Exclunym vs Versonym — Quick Comparison

ExclunymVersonym
Single-purpose wordMultipurpose word
One part of speech onlyMultiple parts of speech
e.g., Javelin (Noun only)e.g., Water (Noun, Adj, Verb)
Named for "exclusive" purposeNamed for "versatile" purpose

Factonym

A creative factual expansion of names into descriptive expressions

A Factonym is a creative exercise where each letter of a person's name (or a country, place, or concept) is expanded into a descriptive word or phrase that captures a factual quality about them.

Factonyms on Famous Persons

Mnemogram

A memory-aid using punctuation in creative, unforgettable ways

A Mnemogram is a creative story or dialogue that demonstrates the power and importance of punctuation marks through memorable, often humorous scenarios.

The Colon Mnemogram

Dad: I want you to marry a girl of my choice.

Son: No.

Dad: The girl is Bill Gates' daughter.

Son: Then ok.

— Dad goes to Bill Gates —

Dad: I want your daughter to marry my son.

Bill Gates: No.

Dad: My son is the CEO of the World Bank.

Bill Gates: Then ok.

— Dad goes to the President of the World Bank —

Dad: Appoint my son as the CEO of your bank.

President: No.

Dad: He is the son-in-law of Bill Gates.

President: Then ok.

💡 Notice how the colon (:) is used after speaker names to introduce dialogue — that's the power of punctuation in action!

Responsive Sentence

Sentences formed without explicit subjects and verbs — a new concept

R U Narasimham identified that in spoken English, we frequently use expressions that lack explicit subjects and verbs, yet convey complete meaning. He coined these as Responsive Sentences.

Examples

Spoken "Happy to see you." → (I am) happy to see you.
Spoken "Nice to talk to you." → (It is) nice to talk to you.
Spoken "Happy Birthday to you." → (I wish) Happy Birthday to you.
Spoken "Feeling great to be here." → (I am) feeling great to be here.

These expressions start with adjectives or present participles, with the implied subject and verb understood from context.
